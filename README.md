#Pawl Pet Personality System — Investor Briefing
What This Is
Pawl's personality assessment is the core intelligence layer of the product. Every personalized recovery plan, every training recommendation, every piece of content the app serves to a pet owner flows from a single upstream input: knowing what kind of animal you're dealing with. Without this layer, Pawl is a generic content app. With it, Pawl becomes a system that learns your pet and gets smarter over time.
The assessment takes roughly five minutes for an owner to complete. By the end of it, the system knows five things about their animal with quantified confidence, maps those five things to a named personality archetype, and immediately delivers a training and care plan that is specific to that animal's behavioral profile. No two outputs are identical.

#How the Science Works
We didn't invent this from scratch. We built on two peer-reviewed frameworks that already exist and are used by veterinary professionals.
For dogs we use the MCPQ-R — the Monash Canine Personality Questionnaire, refined and validated by Ley et al. in 2009 across 450+ animals. It identifies five dimensions that reliably predict how a dog learns, responds to stress, and interacts with its environment. For cats we use the Feline Five, validated by Litchfield et al. in 2017 in a study of over 2,800 cats across three countries. These aren't pop psychology frameworks. They are cited in veterinary and behavioral science journals and are the current standard for applied animal personality assessment.
What we've done is take those frameworks and build a consumer-grade delivery mechanism on top of them — a mobile assessment that any pet owner can complete, scored by a backend that applies the same methodology a behaviorist would use, and wrapped in a personalised narrative generated by an LLM.

#How the Scoring Works
The owner answers 25 questions. Each question is assigned to exactly one of the five personality dimensions before the assessment is ever deployed. That assignment never changes. A question about how a dog responds to strangers affects the Sociability score and nothing else — it does not influence Boldness, Trainability, or any other dimension. This isolation is what makes the scores meaningful and what makes the system auditable.
The questions themselves are designed around behavioral anchors, not opinion statements. We don't ask "is your dog friendly?" We ask "the last time a stranger approached your dog on a walk, what did your dog do?" and give the owner seven specific observable options ranging from "lunged and barked" to "jumped up and sought extended contact." The owner describes what happened. The system scores it. This eliminates the single biggest source of error in owner-reported assessments — personal baseline bias — because the owner is no longer being asked to evaluate their pet, they're being asked to report an event.
Each of the seven response options maps to a point value on a minus-three to plus-three scale. Across five questions per dimension, the maximum score per dimension is fifteen points. That score is converted to a percentage. Any dimension scoring above fifty percent gets its high-end label. Below fifty percent gets the low-end label.

#How the Classification Works
Once the five dimension percentages are locked in, the system doesn't just apply a flat fifty percent cutoff and call it done. That's the naive approach and it creates a real problem — a dog scoring forty-nine percent on Boldness and a dog scoring twenty-two percent on Boldness are completely different animals. Treating them identically produces wrong recommendations and erodes user trust.
Instead we use a three-zone confidence system. Scores between forty-one and fifty-nine percent are classified as borderline — the system acknowledges the ambiguity, shows the owner two possible archetypes, and prompts reassessment in thirty days. Scores between sixty and sixty-nine percent are classified as clear. Scores above seventy percent are dominant — reliable enough to act on with high confidence. Every output comes with a confidence rating. Low confidence outputs tell the owner explicitly not to treat the result as definitive.
The system also runs a coherence check before finalizing the archetype. Certain dimension combinations are statistically rare in the real world. A dog that scores high on both Boldness and Reactivity is an unusual profile — bold dogs tend to be less reactive. If the scores produce an incoherent combination, the system flags it and lowers the confidence rating rather than silently outputting a false-certainty result. This is the kind of thing that distinguishes a clinical tool from a quiz.
After coherence checking, the classification tree runs five sequential if-else decisions — one per dimension — and outputs one of six named archetypes: The Trailblazer, The Companion, The Sentinel, The Gentle Soul, The Free Spirit, or The Observer.

#Why Six Archetypes
Mathematically five binary dimensions produce thirty-two possible combinations. We collapse those into six named archetypes because thirty-two types are too many for a consumer product to be useful — owners need something memorable they can act on. But the nuance isn't lost. The confidence scores and dimension breakdowns are stored and visible to the owner. The six archetypes are the entry point. The dimension scores are the depth layer for users who want it, and the data asset for Pawl as the product scales.

#The Context Modifier Layer
Here is where the product becomes genuinely differentiated for Pawl specifically. A personality score is a baseline. But a pet recovering from surgery, a rescue dog in its first week in a new home, or a twelve-year-old dog with arthritis cannot receive the same training recommendations as a healthy three-year-old of the same archetype. The system collects three pieces of context at intake — age, health status, and ownership history — and applies them as filters on top of the personality output.
A post-surgery dog in active recovery gets all physical training suppressed regardless of its Energy score. A senior dog has high-intensity recommendations removed and cognitive enrichment added. A rescue with unknown history gets a trust-building preamble injected before any formal training recommendations. A dog whose owner reports prior aversive training gets all correction-based methods flagged as contraindicated. None of this overrides the personality profile. It layers on top of it.
This context layer is what connects the personality assessment directly to Pawl's core product — pet recovery. The assessment isn't a standalone feature. It's the intake mechanism for the entire care plan system.

#The Professional Referral Gate
If a dog scores above seventy-five percent on Reactivity or below twenty-five percent on Boldness, the output includes a clear recommendation to consult a certified behaviorist before following the training plan independently. At ninety percent Reactivity, the system escalates to a veterinary behavioral assessment recommendation and flags that medication-assisted behavior modification may be more appropriate than training alone.
This protects the user, the animal, and Pawl. An owner who receives a plan for a severe fear-based dog and executes it incorrectly risks worsening the condition significantly. The referral gate is both an ethical requirement and a liability management mechanism.

#The LLM Layer
The structured output — archetype, confidence score, training plan, modifier flags, referral status — is passed to a locally-run language model that generates the personalised narrative the owner actually reads. This is what makes the result feel like it was written by a behaviorist who knows their specific pet, rather than a template pulled from a database.
The prompt is tightly constrained. The model is instructed to reference the pet's name throughout, acknowledge low confidence outputs explicitly, never recommend aversive methods under any circumstances, and escalate any professional referral flags into the first paragraph. The narrative is not making clinical decisions. The structured scoring layer makes the decisions. The LLM translates them into language an owner can act on.

#The Reassessment Loop
Personality is not fixed, especially in the contexts Pawl operates in — post-surgery, post-adoption, post-health event. The system pushes reassessment prompts at thirty days and ninety days from the initial assessment. If any dimension score shifts by more than twenty points between sessions, the system flags it to the owner and recommends a vet conversation. The ninety-day score is treated as the stable baseline. This gives Pawl a longitudinal data asset and a reason for the owner to keep engaging with the app long after the initial novelty has worn off.

#The Business Case in One Paragraph
What Pawl is building is a system where the more a pet owner uses the product, the more the product knows about their animal, and the more precisely it can serve them. The personality assessment is the flywheel. It generates the archetype that drives the training plan that drives the recovery recommendations that drives the vet referral that drives the subscription retention. Every feature in the app becomes more valuable with a personality profile attached to it. And every reassessment generates new longitudinal data that makes the model smarter and the recommendations more accurate over time. The moat is not the quiz. The moat is the behavioral data that accumulates behind it.
